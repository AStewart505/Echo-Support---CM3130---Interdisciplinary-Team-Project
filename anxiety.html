    Anxiety

    Feeling anxious is a normal response when facing a difficult or unknown situation but once the event has passed, the body relaxes and the feelings of anxiety pass. Symptoms of anxiety differ for everyone but listed below are some of the sensations you may experience:

    • Dry mouth
    • Tearful
    • Nausea
    • Butterflies in the stomach
    • Disturbed sleep
    • Fight or Flight: The survival mechanism - what you are experiencing is the body’s reaction for preparation should you need to respond and react quickly.

    Other forms of anxiety can occur which are longer lasting and can become more problematic if left untreated. Anxieties such as phobias and post-traumatic stress disorder, panic attacks and generalised anxiety disorder (GAD) can begin to affect your ability to study, work and to maintain healthy relationships with others.
    The mental health charity Mind, states that fear of losing control is a factor of anxiety disorders and suggests that working on your self-esteem and self-confidence are strategies to help overcome your fears. For more information visit www.mind.org.uk

    Generalised anxiety: The constant feeling of worrying about nearly anything for no apparent reason is known as generalised anxiety disorder (GAD). You may find yourself worrying needlessly about unknown present or future events which can affect your behaviour in different ways. These can manifest themselves as: avoidance; missing classes, public speaking, interacting with others.

    Panic Attacks: A panic attack can be a frightening experience as the symptoms above can feel more intense and can happen suddenly. These feelings are due to the body’s release of adrenaline and the body being on high alert. NHS Health Scotland states that a panic attack is a sudden onset of intense apprehension, fear or terror.

    Post-traumatic stress disorder: Triggered by witnessing or being involved in a trauma or stressful situation. Common symptoms are having flashbacks or the experience of going through the event once again.

    Phobias: There are a number of different phobias but the most common are: social phobia – fear of taking part in social settings; fear of public speaking and the fear of things such as spiders etc.

    Experiencing other anxieties should not be minimised or ignored as there are treatments available such as talking therapies:

    • Cognitive Behaviour Therapy (CBT) – developing new ways of thinking.
    • Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) - Gradually increasing exposure to the thing that frightens or troubles you, taking action may increase your anxiety but facing up to it will help you to overcome it.
    • Mindfulness – concentrate on the here and now.

    Self Management

    Certain factors such as exhaustion and a poor diet; caffeine and drug misuse can trigger symptoms of anxiety. Adopting a change in lifestyle can improve how you feel. Simple things to try that won’t cost the earth and may save you money.
    For example:

    • Exercise is a great way of busting symptoms of anxiety as the body uses up excess adrenaline.
    • Reduce or cut out stimulants such as caffeine, tobacco and sugar
    • Take time to relax
    • Preparation – don’t leave things to the last minute.

    Complementary therapies such as:

    • Yoga
    • Bach flower remedies
    • Meditation
    • Aromatherapy
